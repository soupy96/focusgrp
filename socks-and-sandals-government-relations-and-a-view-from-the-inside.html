Socks and Sandals, Government Relations and a View from the Inside

by Laura Blondeau
VP, Communications



February 14, 2020

With the wrong approach, those looking to advance their interests can unwittingly shoot themselves in the foot after landing that long sought after meeting with a key decision-maker.

Throughout my years as a senior political staffer, I met with scores of diverse people, interest groups, and public and private sector leadership. One such meeting was with an advocacy group I call ‘Socks and Sandals’ (a nod to their spokesman’s apparel).

From the onset, S&S set a hard line. They threatened to embarrass our government in the media, ‘get’ the opposition parties to badger us, complain to the Ombudsman’s Office and so on, if we didn’t play ball.

The action for which they were advocating was far more left-leaning than our government would ever consider. They either didn’t do their homework about us or thought they could bully their way to success. The meeting ended on an unspoken sour note and we never met with them again. And why would we, given they demonstrated zero interest in exploring common ground?

To varying degrees, the Socks and Sandals approach is more common than you’d expect. But I’ve also seen well-informed, well-meaning folks fail to maximize their limited time with busy politicians and wonder why they aren’t getting ahead.

While there’s much more to it, those looking to advance their interests should:

  • Understand the priorities of the decision-maker and the governing party.
  • Appreciate how the broader political, social and economic climate impacts decision-making.
  • Consider the public good.
  • Understand the gap between what you want and what’s possible. Accepting compromise can build goodwill and lay the groundwork for future advancements.
  • Understand that advocacy is a process and requires patience.
  • We’re all humans here. Be decent to work with.

There’s a saying “The person who acts as their own lawyer has a fool for a client.”

I wouldn’t say ‘foolish’ when it comes to attempting your own GR, but retaining a professional can mean the difference between success and failure. Ask yourself, how badly do you want a win?

Call us. We can help.
Laura Blondeau, VP Communications
