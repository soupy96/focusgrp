Employee Communication and COVID-19: Silence isn't Golden

Laura Blondeau
VP, Communications
March 17, 2020



Concern over COVID-19 accelerated in the public consciousness this month, and for many, the magnitude of the situation truly hit home in the second week of March with the news that Sophie Grégoire Trudeau had contracted the illness. As one colleague put it, “stuff” just got real.

We’ve been watching how various employers have been communicating with their staff during this uncertain time. We’ve had conversations with several clients, leaders, and employees, and listened to their experiences and observations. Here’s a snapshot of the good, the bad and the ugly.

Kudos to the organizations that have been doing detailed pandemic planning for several weeks if not months. They have contingency protocols that have or can be operationalized as needed. They’ve ensured staff who are able to work from home are doing so, and have taken steps to protect those who can’t. Employees are kept informed in a quickly changing situation. And for many, visible leadership exists - the importance of which, cannot be overstated.

On the flip side, as late as March 13, a major company with hundreds of employees across Canada had yet to communicate with workers about COVID-19. For days, staff waited for direction. Some returned to work after out-of-country travel as worry over contamination continued to escalate. This led to friction when some felt their colleagues should have stayed away. In the absence of direction, workers simply didn’t know what to do. Finally, employees pressed their supervisors to allow them to work from home. After employees led the way, a directional memo finally went out.

At another organization, at the end of the second week of March, employees reached out to the administration and requested that leadership establish protocols for employee safety.

In yet another scenario, when prompted for direction in mid-March, a senior leader told managers that the organization would take a “wait and see” approach and align their actions to what other businesses in their sector would do.

At worst, many see this as an extension of how their companies routinely do business - employees simply aren’t informed. That’s a systemic culture problem. At best, it’s fear-based paralysis by well-meaning people who might be in over their heads.

Some mid-sized organizations can lack employee infrastructure such as internal communications staff and a robust human resources department. Leaner operations may focus solely on their core business at the inadvertent expense of employees during difficult times.
